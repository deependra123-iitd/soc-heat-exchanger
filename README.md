This project investigates fouling development in shell-and-tube heat exchangers, explaining why performance often declines suddenly rather than gradually. In industrial settings, dirt and deposits slowly build up on heat transfer surfaces. Performance holds steady for a time, but then efficiency can plummet sharply. To model this, the study applies Self-Organized Criticality (SOC)—a physics concept behind slow-building systems that trigger rare, massive events like earthquakes or forest fires. The heat exchanger is simulated as a grid where fouling accumulates randomly and incrementally at various sites. When fouling at any site surpasses a critical threshold, it "avalanches," redistributing to neighboring areas and potentially triggering a chain reaction. Avalanches vary in size: small ones happen often, while large ones are rare but inevitable. This drives the system toward a critical state, making sudden, major performance drops unavoidable. The key takeaway? Prioritize preventive maintenance to avoid catastrophic failures.
